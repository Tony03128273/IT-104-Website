1. Public Wi‑Fi

Public Wi‑Fi refers to wireless Internet access provided in shared locations such as airports, cafés, hotels, libraries, malls, and campuses. These networks are convenient but often less secure because they may use weak passwords or no encryption at all. On open or poorly configured networks, attackers on the same hotspot can monitor traffic or trick users into connecting to fake networks. Because of this, users should avoid sensitive logins and apply basic protections like VPN and firewall when using public Wi‑Fi.

2. Firewall

A firewall is a security control that filters network traffic to and from a device or network. Personal (host‑based) firewalls help block unsolicited inbound connections on public Wi‑Fi and can alert users to suspicious outbound activity. While a firewall doesn’t encrypt web browsing or replace antivirus, it reduces exposure by limiting what can reach your device. Keeping the firewall enabled alongside system updates and antivirus is a simple, effective baseline defense.

3. VPN

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a trusted server, preventing local eavesdroppers from reading your traffic. On public Wi‑Fi, a VPN mitigates risks from packet sniffing and some man‑in‑the‑middle techniques by shielding the contents of your connection. A VPN does not make unsafe websites secure, so users should still prefer HTTPS and avoid risky downloads. For best results, use a reputable VPN and enable it before logging in to any accounts on public networks.

4. Encryption

Encryption converts readable information into a form that cannot be understood without the correct key. When a website uses HTTPS (TLS), the data exchanged between your browser and the site is encrypted in transit, limiting what attackers can see on public Wi‑Fi. Device‑level encryption also protects stored data if a laptop or phone is lost or stolen. Combining transport encryption (HTTPS/VPN) with device encryption strengthens confidentiality in everyday use.

5. Man‑in‑the‑Middle Attack

A Man‑in‑the‑Middle (MITM) attack occurs when an attacker silently relays or alters communications between two parties who believe they are talking directly. On public Wi‑Fi, MITM can be attempted through rogue access points, ARP spoofing, or DNS manipulation. Strong protections include verifying the network name with staff, using HTTPS‑Only mode and a VPN, and avoiding sensitive actions on open hotspots. If a browser warns about a certificate error, do not proceed—this can be a sign of interception.
